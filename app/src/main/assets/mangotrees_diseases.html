6.DISEASES OF MANGO TREES
There are several diseases that can affect mango trees, some of which
are:
1.Anthracnose - This is a fungal disease that affects mango trees,
causing black spots on the fruit, leaves, and flowers. The disease can
spread
rapidly in warm and humid weather.
=>Anthracnose disease in mango trees typically occurs during periods of
warm and humid weather. The disease is caused by the fungal pathogen
Colletotrichum gloeosporioides and can affect all parts of the tree,
including the leaves, flowers, and fruit. Early symptoms of anthracnose
include small, water-soaked lesions on the leaves and fruit, which can
enlarge and turn dark brown or black over time. The fruit may also become
sunken and have a soft, rotting texture. Severe infection can cause
defoliation and significant yield loss. Control measures for anthracnose
disease include the use of fungicides, good cultural practices such as
sanitation and pruning to improve air circulation and reduce disease
pressure, and the removal and destruction of infected plant material.
Early detection and control are essential to minimize the impact of the
disease.
2.Powdery mildew - This is a fungal disease that affects mango leaves
and causes a white powdery coating on them. The disease can reduce the
photosynthetic capacity of the leaves and lead to reduced growth and
yield.
=>Powdery mildew disease in mango trees usually occurs during periods of
high humidity and moderate temperatures. The disease is caused by fungal
pathogens belonging to the genus Oidium, which can infect the leaves and
other parts of the tree. Early symptoms of powdery mildew include a white
or gray powdery coating on the surface of the leaves, stems, and flowers.
The leaves may become distorted and stunted, and the disease can also
reduce the photosynthetic capacity of the leaves, leading to reduced
growth and yield. Control measures for powdery mildew disease include the
use of fungicides, good cultural practices such as sanitation and pruning
to improve air circulation and reduce disease pressure, and the removal
and destruction of infected plant material. Early detection and control
are essential to minimize the impact of the disease.
3.Mango malformation - This is a physiological disorder that affects the
flower and fruit development in mango trees. The disease can cause
abnormal growth of flowers, resulting in malformed and small fruit.
=>Mango malformation disease is a physiological disorder that affects
flower and fruit development in mango trees. The disease is caused by a
complex interaction between genetic and environmental factors, and its
exact cause is not fully understood. The disease can affect all parts of
the tree, but the flowers are the most commonly affected. The flowers may
develop abnormally, with a proliferation of sterile, malformed flowers
and a reduction in the number of fertile flowers. As a result, the fruit
may be small, misshapen, and of poor quality. In severe cases, the
disease can cause dieback of branches and reduced growth and yield.
Control measures for mango malformation disease include the use of
disease-free planting material, pruning infected branches, and the
application of growth regulators such as paclobutrazol to regulate plant
growth and flower production. However, prevention is the most effective
approach to control the disease, by choosing cultivars that are less
susceptible to the disease and maintaining good orchard management
practices.
4.Bacterial black spot - This is a bacterial disease that affects the
fruit and leaves of mango trees, causing black spots on the fruit and
defoliation.
=>Bacterial black spot disease in mango trees is caused by the bacterium
Xanthomonas campestris. The disease can affect all parts of the tree, but
it is most commonly found on the leaves and fruit. Early symptoms of
bacterial black spot include small, water-soaked lesions on the leaves,
which can enlarge and turn black over time. The fruit may also develop
black spots that are sunken and have a soft, rotting texture. Severe
infection can cause defoliation and significant yield loss. The disease
spreads rapidly in warm and humid weather and can be transmitted by rain,
wind, and insect vectors. Control measures for bacterial black spot
disease include the use of copper-based fungicides, good cultural
practices such as sanitation and pruning to improve air circulation and
reduce disease pressure, and the removal and destruction of infected
plant material. Early detection and control are essential to minimize the
impact of the disease.
5.Fruit fly infestation - This is an insect pest that can cause
significant damage to mango fruit, leading to premature fruit drop and
reduced yield.
=>Fruit fly infestation is a common pest problem that affects mango
trees. The most common species of fruit flies that attack mangoes are the
Oriental fruit fly (Bactrocera dorsalis) and the Mediterranean fruit fly
(Ceratitis capitata). The adult flies lay their eggs under the skin of
the fruit, and the developing larvae feed on the pulp, causing the fruit
to rot and drop prematurely. Early symptoms of fruit fly infestation
include the appearance of puncture marks or oviposition scars on the
surface of the fruit, and the presence of small white maggots inside the
fruit. Infested fruits may also emit a strong odor and develop a soft,
mushy texture. Control measures for fruit fly infestation include the use
of insecticides, physical barriers such as netting or bags to protect the
fruit, and the removal and destruction of infested fruit. Regular
monitoring and early detection are essential to control the spread of the
pest and minimize the impact on fruit production.
6.Mango sudden death - This is a disease that can cause sudden death of
mango trees, with no visible symptoms until the tree dies. The disease is
caused by a fungus and is known to affect mango trees in Brazil.
=>Mango sudden death disease (MSDD) is a serious disease that affects
mango trees and can cause significant yield loss. The disease is caused
by a phytoplasma, a type of bacteria-like organism that infects the
phloem tissue of the tree. The exact cause and transmission of MSDD is
not yet fully understood, but it is believed to be spread by insect
vectors such as leafhoppers and planthoppers. Early symptoms of MSDD
include yellowing and wilting of the leaves, followed by sudden death of
the tree within a few weeks or months. Infected trees do not respond to
treatment, and there is currently no known cure for the disease. Control
measures for MSDD include the removal and destruction of infected trees
and the use of disease-free planting material. Early detection and
monitoring of insect populations can also help to prevent the spread of
the disease. It is important to practice good orchard management
practices, such as regular pruning and sanitation, to reduce the risk of
MSDD and other diseases in mango trees.
