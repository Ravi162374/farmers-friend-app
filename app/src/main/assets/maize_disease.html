4.DISEASES OF MAIZE
Maize is susceptible to a number of diseases. Some common diseases that
affect maize crop are:
1.Maize smut: This is a fungal disease caused by the fungus Ustilago
maydis. It causes swollen galls to form on the ears, tassels, and other
parts of
the plant. These galls are filled with dark, powdery spores.
=>When Maize smut disease occurs in maize crops, it causes swollen galls
to form on the ears, tassels, and other parts of the plant. These galls
are filled with dark, powdery spores. The spores can be easily spread by
wind, rain, or contact with farm tools or clothing. The disease can lead
to significant yield losses, and the infected ears are not suitable for
consumption or processing.
To manage Maize smut disease in maize crops, farmers can use several
strategies. These include planting resistant varieties of maize, rotating
maize with other crops, using certified seed that is free from smut, and
removing and destroying infected plant debris. In addition, fungicides
can be applied as a preventive measure or to control the spread of the
disease. Farmers can also adopt cultural practices such as crop rotation,
intercropping with legumes, and maintaining proper plant spacing and soil
moisture to reduce the likelihood of infection.
2.Gray leaf spot: This is a fungal disease caused by the fungus
Cercospora zeae-maydis. It causes grayish-green, rectangular lesions on
the leaves
that turn brown with age. Severe infections can cause defoliation and
yield losses.
=>When Gray leaf spot disease occurs in maize crops, it causes small,
rectangular-shaped lesions to form on the leaves, which may later
coalesce to form larger, grayish-brown lesions. The disease can reduce
photosynthesis and cause premature leaf drop, leading to reduced yields.
It can also increase the risk of other diseases and pests attacking the
plant.
To manage Gray leaf spot disease in maize crops, farmers can use several
strategies. These include planting resistant varieties of maize, crop
rotation, tillage, and maintaining proper plant spacing and soil moisture
to reduce the likelihood of infection. Fungicides can also be used to
control the spread of the disease, particularly if it has already been
observed in the field. Cultural practices such as removing infected plant
debris and avoiding overhead irrigation can also help prevent the spread
of the disease.
3.Northern corn leaf blight: This is a fungal disease caused by the
fungus Exserohilum turcicum. It causes cigar-shaped lesions on the leaves
that
turn brown with age. Severe infections can cause defoliation and yield
losses.
=>When Northern corn leaf blight disease occurs in maize crops, it causes
cigar-shaped lesions to form on the leaves. These lesions are gray-green
to tan and can be up to several inches long. As the disease progresses,
the lesions may turn gray or tan and produce dark, fuzzy spores. Northern
corn leaf blight can reduce photosynthesis and lead to significant yield
losses.
To manage Northern corn leaf blight disease in maize crops, farmers can
use several strategies. These include planting resistant varieties of
maize, rotating maize with other crops, and avoiding excessive nitrogen
fertilization, which can increase susceptibility to the disease.
Fungicides can also be used to control the spread of the disease,
particularly if it has already been observed in the field. Cultural
practices such as removing infected plant debris and avoiding overhead
irrigation can also help prevent the spread of the disease.
4.Southern corn leaf blight: This is a fungal disease caused by the
fungus Bipolaris maydis. It causes oval-shaped lesions on the leaves that
turn tan
or gray with age. Severe infections can cause defoliation and yield
losses.
=>When Southern corn leaf blight disease occurs in maize crops, it causes
oval-shaped lesions to form on the leaves. These lesions are tan in color
and may have a darker border. The disease can spread rapidly and cause
significant damage to the leaves, reducing photosynthesis and leading to
yield losses.
To manage Southern corn leaf blight disease in maize crops, farmers can
use several strategies. These include planting resistant varieties of
maize, crop rotation, and avoiding excessive nitrogen fertilization,
which can increase susceptibility to the disease. Fungicides can also be
used to control the spread of the disease, particularly if it has already
been observed in the field. Cultural practices such as removing infected
plant debris and avoiding overhead irrigation can also help prevent the
spread of the disease.
5.Maize dwarf mosaic virus: This is a viral disease that is transmitted
by aphids. It causes yellow streaking and mottling on the leaves,
stunting of the
plant, and reduced yield.
=>When Maize dwarf mosaic virus disease occurs in maize crops, it causes
stunted growth, yellowing or mottling of the leaves, and reduced yield.
The virus is transmitted by aphids and can infect plants at any stage of
growth.
To manage Maize dwarf mosaic virus disease in maize crops, farmers can
use several strategies. These include planting virus-free seed,
controlling aphid populations through the use of insecticides, and
removing infected plants from the field. Crop rotation with non-host
plants can also help reduce the spread of the virus. There are no
chemical treatments available to control Maize dwarf mosaic virus, so
preventive measures are critical for disease management.
6.Fusarium ear rot: This is a fungal disease caused by the fungus
Fusarium verticillioides. It causes pink or white mold to develop on the
ears, which
can produce toxins that are harmful to humans and livestock.
=>When Fusarium ear rot disease occurs in maize crops, it can cause
significant yield losses and can also produce mycotoxins that are harmful
to both humans and animals. Symptoms of the disease include white to
pinkish fungal growth on the ears, premature ear drop, and ear rot that
may extend into the kernels.
To manage Fusarium ear rot disease in maize crops, farmers can use
several strategies. These include planting resistant varieties of maize,
crop rotation with non-host plants, avoiding plant stress through proper
irrigation and fertilization, and managing insect pests such as the corn
earworm, which can create entry points for the fungus. Fungicides can
also be used to control the spread of the disease, but their
effectiveness may be limited once the fungus has infected the ear.
Harvesting infected ears early can also help reduce mycotoxin
contamination, although it may not prevent yield losses. It is important
to properly store and handle harvested grain to prevent further
contamination by mycotoxins produced by the fungus.
To manage these diseases in maize, it is important to use integrated pest
management practices that include a combination of cultural, mechanical,
and chemical control measures. This can include planting resistant
varieties, crop rotation, maintaining proper plant spacing and soil
moisture, and applying fungicides and insecticides as needed.
